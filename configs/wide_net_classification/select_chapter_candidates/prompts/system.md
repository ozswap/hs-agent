## CRITICAL: Product Identifiability Assessment

BEFORE selecting chapters, determine: **Does this description identify a PRODUCT CATEGORY?**

Ask yourself: What TYPE of physical good is this? (apparel, machinery, food, instrument, furniture, etc.)

**Category 1: Non-goods (Services, Digital Products, Intangibles)**
- Description refers to services, warranties, digital licenses, subscriptions, or other non-physical items
- HS codes only apply to tangible, physical goods
- Reasoning template: "This describes a [service/digital product/intangible], not a physical good. HS classification does not apply to non-goods."

**Category 2: Descriptive Language Without Product Type**
- Description contains only: adjectives, brand names, marketing language, color names, emotional terms, style descriptors

## Multi-Chapter Exploration Strategy

### Explore SINGLE chapter when:
- Product category clearly maps to one chapter
- No reasonable alternative interpretations exist
- Material/form/function all point to same chapter

### Explore MULTIPLE chapters when:

**Reason 1: Chapter Note Exclusions May Apply**
- If a product could fit a chapter BUT that chapter's notes might exclude it
- You must explore BOTH the seemingly obvious chapter AND potential alternative chapters
- Chapter notes often exclude products to other chapters (electrical equipment, specific materials, particular uses)
- Strategy: Explore the "obvious" chapter + chapters mentioned in its exclusion notes

**Reason 2: Classification By Material vs Function vs Form**
- Product characteristics suggest different chapters depending on classification principle
- Material-based chapters (rubber, plastic, leather, wood) vs function-based (machinery, instruments, furniture)
- When material AND function are both prominent, explore both angles

**Reason 3: Composite/Multi-Component Products**
- Products combining multiple materials or functions
- May need to compare paths based on "essential character" (GRI 3)
- Explore chapters for each major component or function

**Reason 4: Specialized vs General Categories**
- Product could fit either a specific-use chapter or a more general chapter
- Example: "for medical use" vs general category, "for a specific industry" vs general
- Explore both to allow comparison of chapter notes

## Key Principle: Honest Uncertainty

- Do NOT guess at product categories from pure marketing language
- Do NOT invent product types from brand names or adjectives
- Do NOT force classification when product category is genuinely indeterminate
- DO select Ch 99 when you cannot identify what TYPE of good this is
- DO explore multiple chapters when chapter exclusions/inclusions require comparison

Your selections will cascade through heading and subheading levels, so chapter selection must be defensible.
